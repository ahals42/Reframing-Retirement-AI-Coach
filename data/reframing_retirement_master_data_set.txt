LESSON 1: Why Physical Activity Matters During Retirement (35 slides)
=====================================================================
L01-S01-G001 | Lesson 1 Slide 1/35 | Global 1/424

Lesson Description: This lesson shows why physical activity is important during retirement and how it benefits your health, independence, and quality of life.

*********************************************************************
L01-S02-G002 | Lesson 1 Slide 2/35 | Global 2/424

Title: What is physical activity?
+++

Content:
Physical activity means any movement of your body that uses energy. The World Health Organization describes it as “any bodily movement produced by your muscles that requires energy expenditure.” Physical activity can happen anywhere at home, outdoors, or in your community. It can look different for everyone, depending on your health, mobility, and interests.

*********************************************************************
L01-S03-G003 | Lesson 1 Slide 3/35 | Global 3/424

Title: How to be active
+++

Content:
Every move counts, from stretching or lifting groceries to walking, dancing, or using resistance bands to build strength!

Leisure activities like walking, swimming, or cycling

Active travel, such as walking or wheeling to the store

Domestic activities like cleaning, gardening, or home repairs

Enjoyable movement like dancing, playing with grandchildren, or joining a recreation group

*********************************************************************
L01-S04-G004 | Lesson 1 Slide 4/35 | Global 4/424

Title: Let's talk intensity
+++

Content:
Some activities make your heart beat faster, others build strength or balance!

-   Light activity

-   Moderate-to-vigorous physical activity

-   Muscle and bone strengthening activities

-   Balance activities

Let’s take a look at each!

*********************************************************************
L01-S05-G005 | Lesson 1 Slide 5/35 | Global 5/424

Title: Light activity
+++

Content:
Light activity: Movement that feels easy and comfortable. You can talk easily and aren’t short of breath (e.g., slow walking, stretching, light gardening).

*********************************************************************
L01-S06-G006 | Lesson 1 Slide 6/35 | Global 6/424

Title: Moderate-to-vigorous physical activity
+++

Content:
Moderate-to-vigorous physical activity: Movement that is intense enough to increase your heart rate and elevate your body temperature (e.g., running, jumping rope, tennis, swimming).

*********************************************************************
L01-S07-G007 | Lesson 1 Slide 7/35 | Global 7/424

Title: Muscle- and bone- strengthening activities
+++

Content:
Muscle and bone strengthening activities: Activities that increase skeletal muscle strength, power, endurance, and mass (e.g., resistance training and heavy gardening involving digging or shovelling). Bone-strengthening activities produce an impact or tension force on the bones that promotes bone growth and strength (e.g., running, jumping rope, and lifting weights).

*********************************************************************
L01-S08-G008 | Lesson 1 Slide 8/35 | Global 8/424

Title: A closer look: Strengthening activities (Video)
+++

Content:
There's no single 'best' way to build muscle or strength. While heavier weights and more sets can help build strength. Many different ways of doing strength exercise can be beneficial. The key is finding a type of movement you enjoy - and can stick with over time. That might be lifting weights, using resistance bands, or doing body-weight movements like squats, wall pull-ups or yoga. Even low intensity activities - like stretching - can lead to small improvements in strength and flexibility. Resistance training to a 15% lower risk of death from any cause, and a 19% lower risk of cardiovascular related death.

*********************************************************************
L01-S09-G009 | Lesson 1 Slide 9/35 | Global 9/424

Title: Balance
+++

Content:
Balance exercises: Static and dynamic balance exercises are designed to improve your ability to maintain balance while walking on your own or in a crowd, in bad weather, while climbing up and down steps, or opening heavy doors. Activities that improve balance include walking on uneven ground (e.g., unpaved areas, forest trails), Tai Chi, and yoga.

*********************************************************************
L01-S10-G010 | Lesson 1 Slide 10/35 | Global 10/424

Title: Movements that supports healthy aging
+++

Content:
According to the World Health Organization, for adults and older adults, regular physical activity is associated with:

-   Reduced risk of all-cause mortality

-   Lower risk of cardiovascular disease, hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers

-   Improved mental health, cognitive function, sleep quality, and body composition

-   Fewer falls and fall-related injuries

*********************************************************************
L01-S11-G011 | Lesson 1 Slide 11/35 | Global 11/424

Title: Why this matters now
+++

Content:
Increased mobility

Falls remain the leading cause of injury-related hospitalizations among older adults living in Canada. Between 20% and 30% of older adults fall each year.

Thankfully, activities like strength training or brisk walking help you reach the recommended 150 minutes of weekly moderate-to-vigorous activity while also improving balance, core strength, and stability—key factors in preventing falls.

*********************************************************************
L01-S12-G012 | Lesson 1 Slide 12/35 | Global 12/424

Title: Why this matters now
+++

Content:
Cognitive wellness

By 2031, nearly 1.4 million Canadians are projected to live with dementia, with direct health-care costs reaching about $16.6 billion.

Because age is the main risk factor for dementia, the number of Canadians living with it is expected to rise. The good news? Physical activity can help prevent dementia and slow its progression—supporting both your body and your brain.

*********************************************************************
L01-S13-G013 | Lesson 1 Slide 13/35 | Global 13/424

Title: Why this matters now
+++

Content:
Connection and community

Research indicates that 1 in 5 adults living in Canada experience some level of loneliness or isolation.

Older adults are most at risk due to reduced mobility, falls, and shrinking social networks. Regular physical activity boosts mood, fosters social connections, and helps keep the brain healthy.

*********************************************************************

Higher energy vs fatigue

*********************************************************************
L01-S15-G015 | Lesson 1 Slide 15/35 | Global 15/424

Title: Is it ever to late to start exercising (Video)
+++

Content:
Is it ever too late to start an exercise program? Absolutely not. It doesn’t matter what your current fitness level is or how old you are—the benefits of exercise are undeniable. There are two important things to keep in mind. First, you need to set reasonable goals and gradually ramp things up. This approach sets you up for success and helps reduce the risk of injury. A strong exercise program is built on four key pillars: Stability training – This helps prevent falls and supports functional fitness, allowing you to move confidently in daily life. Resistance training – Strength training helps combat the natural loss of muscle that comes with aging. Muscle is often called the organ of longevity because of how important it is for long-term health. Cardiovascular training – Measures like VO₂ max, which reflect how efficiently your heart works, are direct predictors of longevity. Cardio exercise is also one of the few things proven to maintain and improve cognitive function and mental health. Nutrition – A solid nutrition program provides the building blocks your body needs to adapt, recover, and succeed. Starting can be hard. Staying consistent can be hard. But becoming frail is hard too. Living without strength is hard. Choosing movement is choosing the better kind of hard.

For health benefits, adults - including those aged 65 and older - should be physically active every day, minimize sedentary time, and get enough good-quality sleep. This is the long-term goal that we are aiming for, but we can always start little by little!

Title: What is mood?
+++

Content:
Think of mood as your emotional “background music.” It’s a steady state, like feeling calm, happy, sad, or anxious, that lingers in the background of your day. Unlike brief emotions that flare up and fade quickly, mood can last for hours or even days, shaping how you think, see the world, and connect with others. Mood shifts are completely normal. Ups, downs, and everything in between are just part of being human.

*********************************************************************

Title: Anxiety and depression explained
+++

Content:
Anxiety: A state of worry, nervousness, or unease. It can affect your sleep, focus, and energy. Occasional anxiety is normal, but ongoing anxiety can make daily life more difficult.

Depression: More than just sadness. It’s a persistent low mood or loss of interest in things you usually enjoy. Depression can affect your motivation, sleep, appetite, and overall energy.

Title: A closer look: Confidence and physical activity (Video)
+++

Content: 
People who feel more confident about their ability to be active usually end up doing more physical activity overall. In research, this type of confidence is called self-efficacy. Self-efficacy means your perceived capability to perform a behaviour - for physical activity, its about how confident you are that you can do the activity at hand. In fact, high levels of self-efficacy often distinguish someone who is active from someone who is not. It's important not to confuse capability with motivation. We often say, "I can't" when we really mean "I don't want to." But the opposite is also true - even if you want to be active, it's hard to follow through if you don't feel confident you can actually do it.

SB01-S03-G097 | Part 1 Slide 3/29 | Global 97/424

Title: Behind the science M PAC theoretical framework (VIDEO)
+++

Content:
We all start with good intentions - like, "I'm going to be more active for now on." But sticking with It over time...thats the real challenge. This is where the MPAC framework comes in, MPAC stands for multi-process action control, developed through physical activity research lab at the University of Victoria Behavioural Medicine Lab. MPAC helps explain how we move... from deciding to be active... to keeping it up over time. It does this by three phases that work together; reflexive, regulatory and reflective. The reflective phase is about our conscious thoughts - the decision we make about being active. This phase is about our basic thoughts and abilities towards behaviour we want to change or incorporate. For example: Do we think it's worth it? Will it feel good? Do we have time or confidence to do it. When these beliefs and abilities are positive, we form intentions to get moving. The regulatory phase helps us endeavour - bridging the gap between intention and action. This is the phase where you set goals, create plans, and problem solve and set yourself up for success. Where you monitor and track your behaviour to stay on course. And, use positive self-talk to reinforce successes and cope with setbacks. In other words, the regulatory phase is the glue that helps our intentions turn into real behaviour. Over time, as we repeat a behaviour the reflexive phase kicks in - helping us sustain it. This is when the behaviour becomes part of your life and regular routine. It begins to feel easier and more natural - supported by habits and identity. Habits help make activity more automatic by using prompts and cues. Identity is about making it part of who you are - by finding personal meaning in the behaviour. In other words the reflexive phase is what seals the behaviour for the long term. So, MPAC isn't just about starting something, it's about keeping it up over time. By understanding and supporting these three phases, we can just turn motivation into meaningful lasting behaviour.

Title: What is self-monitoring?
+++

Content:
Self-monitoring means keeping track of your own behaviour so that you can reflect, learn, and make changes when needed.

It’s the ongoing process of observing and evaluating one’s thoughts, feelings, and behaviours. It can be dynamic (checking in as things are happening) or retrospective (looking back on what you did).

It can take two main forms:

-   Internal self-monitoring

-   External self-monitoring

-   External self-monitoring: Recording what you did, when, and for how long, using a device or written record.

+++

-   Ticking off each time you play pickleball on a calendar

-   Checking your Apple Watch, Fitbit, or pedometer to see daily steps

Title: A closer look: Benefits of self monitoring (Video)
+++

Content: 
Self-monitoring can be especially valuable during retirement, when daily routines may be less structured that during work life. It increased awareness of daily activity levels. It provides a sense of progress and achievement. It encourages consistency in routines like walking, stretching, hiking, or social sports such as pickleball. It creates structure in unstructured time, helping physical activity become part of the day. It makes physical activity visible, which strengthens motivation. LESSON 7: The Habit Recipe: Cue, Routine, Repeat (39 slides)
============================================================
L07-S01-G226 | Lesson 7 Slide 1/39 | Global 226/424

Lesson Description: This lesson will provide you with practical tools and tactics to help you build habits that support physical activity.

*********************************************************************
L07-S02-G227 | Lesson 7 Slide 2/39 | Global 227/424

Title: What are habits?
+++

Content:
When most people think about habits, they imagine something they “have” — like “I have a habit of walking every morning.”

But researchers explain habits differently.

A habit is not the behaviour itself. It’s the automatic process that connects a cue in your environment to a behavioural response.

*********************************************************************
L07-S03-G228 | Lesson 7 Slide 3/39 | Global 228/424

Title: Habits trigger behaviour
+++

Content:
Habits are cue-response associations

When you encounter the cue (like brushing your teeth or finishing breakfast), your brain automatically generates an impulse to act

This process requires much less mental effort than making a fresh decision every time

Key point: Habits don’t replace behaviour - they trigger behaviour

*********************************************************************
L07-S04-G229 | Lesson 7 Slide 4/39 | Global 229/424

Title: Instigation vs execution 
+++

Content:
Physical activity is a complex behaviour, which makes it hard to run on autopilot.

Instead, researchers highlight two phases:

-   Instigation: Deciding to start (cue-based and often automatic)

-   Execution: Carrying it all out (still needs planning, effort, or regulation)

Habits matter most at the instigation stage. They help you cross the hardest threshold: getting started!

Let’s take a look at examples of each phase!

*********************************************************************
L07-S05-G230 | Lesson 7 Slide 5/39 | Global 230/424

Title: Instigation vs execution examples 
+++

Content:
Instigation habit:

After I finish my coffee (cue), I put on my walking shoes

Execution:

I still need to choose my route and decide how far to go

So, habits don’t do the whole job — but they remove the biggest barrier by getting you moving.

*********************************************************************
L07-S06-G231 | Lesson 7 Slide 6/39 | Global 231/424

Title: A closer look: Habits aren't behaviours (Video)
+++

Content:
It's easy to fall into the trap of thinking that the behaviour is the habit. People often say things like, "Walking everyday is my habit," or "I broke my habit of snacking at night." But this way of thinking can be misleading. Habits are inputs into routine, not the whole routine. Habits work by being triggered by cues - like after lunch - which is why old behaviours can return when familiar cues reappear - even after change. If we confuse habits with behaviours, it can look like a habit is "broken" when someone stops doing the behaviour. In reality, the habit association can still exist and be reactivated when the same cues are still present. Understanding habits this way can help explain lapses - and make it easier to respond with flexibility rather than self-blame.

*********************************************************************
L07-S07-G232 | Lesson 7 Slide 7/39 | Global 232/424

Title: Why habits are important
+++

Content:
Habits free up mental energy, so staying active requires less conscious effort.

Once established, habits are persistent — they tend to “stick” even when motivation is low.

Habits are context-dependent: the stronger the link between cue and behaviour, and the more often the cue appears, the more reliable the behaviour becomes.

Title: Is it true it takes 66 days to form a habit?
+++

Content:
Short answer: there’s no single
magic number.
The “66 days” figure comes
from one study, but the real
time varies a lot from person to
person and from behaviour to
behaviour.
Let’s take a closer look:
96 volunteers chose a daily
behaviour (like drinking water
with lunch or walking after
breakfast) and repeated it in
the same context for 12
weeks.

SB03-S07-G405 | Part 3 Slide 7/26 | Global 405/424

Title: More about the habit study
+++

Content:
They tracked whether they did
it and how automatic it felt,
using the Self-Report Habit
Index.
Habit strength increased
quickly at first, then levelled
off — following what scientists
call an “asymptotic curve.
”
Reaching a strong level of
automaticity took anywhere

depending on the person and
behaviour.
Missing one day didn’t
disrupt the process.

Title: Recap: What is affect?
+++

Content:
Researchers use the word
affect to describe simple
feeling states — our moods
and emotions. Moods are longer-lasting
feeling states that don’t
always have a clear cause
(e.g., feeling generally
upbeat, or “in a funk” all day).
Emotions are short-term
feeling states linked to
specific events (e.g., happy
after good news, frustrated
when plans are cancelled).
